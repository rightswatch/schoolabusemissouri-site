Types of School Abuse Covered Under Missouri Law

Overview of the types of abuse that can give rise to civil claims against Missouri schools and institutions — sexual abuse, physical abuse, bullying, and institutional failures.

By Rights Watch Media Group • January 1, 0001 • 2 min read

Sexual Abuse by School Employees

Sexual abuse by teachers, coaches, counselors, administrators, bus drivers, and other school employees is the most serious category. Missouri law provides:

  • No statute of limitations for civil claims under § 537.046
  • Criminal liability for the perpetrator under § 566.083 and related statutes
  • Institutional liability against the district for negligent hiring, supervision, or retention

Red flags include: private one-on-one meetings, grooming behavior (gifts, special treatment), preferential academic treatment, secrecy, and contact outside normal school activities.

Sexual Harassment (Peer-on-Peer)

Student-on-student sexual harassment can give rise to Title IX claims against the school when:

  • A school official with authority knew about the harassment
  • The school’s response was clearly unreasonable (“deliberate indifference”)
  • The harassment was severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive

Physical Abuse

Corporal punishment is still legal in Missouri. However, excessive physical discipline by school employees that causes injury, or physical abuse unrelated to discipline, can give rise to:

  • Civil claims against the district
  • Criminal charges against the employee
  • Missouri DESE disciplinary action

Bullying and Cyberbullying

Missouri’s anti-bullying law (§ 160.775) requires school districts to adopt anti-bullying policies. Severe bullying that rises to the level of creating a hostile educational environment may trigger Title IX if it is based on sex.

Institutional Failures

Beyond direct abuse, civil claims may arise from a school’s:

  • Failure to screen employees for prior abuse history (negligent hiring)
  • Failure to investigate after receiving a report (deliberate indifference)
  • Transferring a known abuser to another school (“pass the trash”)
  • Suppressing or covering up reports to protect the institution’s reputation
  • Retaliating against students or families who report
