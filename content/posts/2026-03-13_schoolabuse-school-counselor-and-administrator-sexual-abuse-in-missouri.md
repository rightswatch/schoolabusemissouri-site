- **Teachers** — Daily access to students, private classroom settings, and authority that creates inherent power imbalances make teacher-on-student abuse one of the most commonly reported categories of school sexual misconduct. Under the deliberate indifference standard established in *Gebser v. - **School Counselors** — Counselors present a particular institutional risk because their professional role explicitly involves private, emotionally vulnerable conversations with students. When a counselor exploits that access, and the employing public school district or private K-12 school had actual knowledge under *Gebser* and responded with deliberate indifference, federal Title IX liability attaches. Every school counselor employed by a Missouri public school district or private K-12 school is also an individual mandatory reporter under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 210.115 — meaning that any other school employee who suspects the counselor is abusing a student must report that suspicion immediately to the Missouri Children's Division or local law enforcement, without waiting for certainty and without deferring to the counselor's institutional role.

## Federal Law: What Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 Requires Missouri Schools to Do

### The Governing Statute

Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, codified at 20 U.S.C. § 1681, prohibits discrimination on the basis of sex in any education program or activity receiving federal financial assistance. Every public school district in Missouri receives federal financial assistance and is covered by Title IX. Private K-12 schools and boarding schools that accept federal funding are equally covered. The U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights and federal courts have long held that sexual harassment and sexual abuse constitute sex-based discrimination within the meaning of 20 U.S.C. § 1681.

### The Deliberate Indifference Standard Under *Gebser* and *Davis*

Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681, does not impose automatic liability on a public school district or private institution every time an employee or student commits sexual misconduct. The United States Supreme Court has established the specific standards that govern when a school can be held financially responsible in a civil **Title IX lawsuit in Missouri**.

**Teacher-on-Student and Employee-on-Student Abuse — *Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School District*, 524 U.S. 274 (1998)**

In *Gebser*, the Supreme Court held that a school district can be held liable under Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681, for a teacher's sexual abuse of a student when two conditions are met:

1. A school official with authority to institute corrective measures had **actual knowledge** of the employee's misconduct, and
2. The district's response to that knowledge amounted to **deliberate indifference**

Constructive knowledge — what the public school district "should have known" through reasonable supervision — does not satisfy *Gebser*. A school official with actual authority to act must have actually known. That said, actual knowledge under *Gebser* does not require the institution to have directly witnessed the abuse. Reports communicated to a responsible official by students, parents, teachers, or other school employees constitute actual knowledge when those reports reach a person with authority to institute corrective measures.

When a school counselor or administrator is the perpetrator, the *Gebser* analysis focuses on which official outside the perpetrator received a report of the misconduct, and what that official did — or failed to do — in response. This inquiry is particularly significant in Missouri because Mo. Rev. Stat. § 210.115 independently required any school employee who received that same information to report it immediately to the Missouri Children's Division or local law enforcement. A school official's failure to satisfy that mandatory reporting obligation is direct evidence relevant to the **deliberate indifference Title IX** analysis under *Gebser*.

**Peer-on-Peer Harassment — *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999)**

In *Davis*, the Supreme Court held that a public school district is liable under Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681, for student-on-student sexual harassment when:

1. The district had **actual knowledge** of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive harassment,
2. The district was **deliberately indifferent** to that harassment, and
3. The harassment was so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it effectively deprived the victim of access to educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school
