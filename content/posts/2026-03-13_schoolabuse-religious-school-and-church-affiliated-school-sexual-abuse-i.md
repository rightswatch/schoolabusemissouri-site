## Does Title IX Apply to Religious Schools in Missouri?

### The Answer Depends on Federal Funding

Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, 20 U.S.C. § 1681, prohibits sex discrimination — including sexual harassment and sexual violence — in educational programs that receive **federal financial assistance**. Many Missouri religious private schools receive such funding in forms that never appear in tuition contracts or enrollment materials:

- Federal nutrition programs, including the National School Lunch Program and School Breakfast Program
- Federal special education funding under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), 20 U.S.C. § 1400 et seq.
- Federal Title I literacy and educational support grants administered through Missouri DESE
- Federal transportation or other categorical grants passed through Missouri state agencies
- Federal student financial aid programs at the postsecondary level, including Pell Grants and federal student loans

If a Missouri religious private school accepts any of these funds, it is generally a covered recipient under Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681, subject to the full range of its anti-discrimination and anti-harassment obligations. Determining whether a specific school receives covered funding is one of the first things a **school district liability Missouri** attorney will investigate when evaluating a Title IX lawsuit Missouri families are considering.

### The Religious Exemption Under Title IX — and Its Limits

Title IX contains a specific exemption for religious institutions under **20 U.S.C. § 1681(a)(3)**. The statute provides that Title IX does not apply to an educational institution "controlled by a religious organization" to the extent that compliance would "not be consistent with the religious tenets of such organization."

This exemption has real force. It also has limits Missouri families need to understand before assuming a religious school escapes Title IX accountability:

- The institution must be **actually controlled** by a religious organization — not merely affiliated with one, carrying a religious name, or holding occasional chapel services
- The exemption applies **only to the extent that compliance with Title IX conflicts with specific, identified religious tenets** — it is not a blanket shield against all Title IX obligations
- The institution must **affirmatively claim** the exemption by notifying the U.S. Department of Education Office for Civil Rights — the exemption is not automatic simply because a school has a religious identity
- **The sexual abuse of children is not a protected religious tenet** under any legally cognizable interpretation, and no Missouri religious institution has successfully argued otherwise in federal court

**Practical reality for Missouri families:** Many Missouri Catholic schools, Lutheran academies, and evangelical Christian private schools that accept federal nutrition or special education funding have never affirmatively claimed the Title IX religious exemption with OCR. Those institutions may be fully subject to Title IX. Whether a specific school claimed the exemption, when it claimed it, and whether that claim was procedurally and substantively valid are fact-specific questions requiring review of the school's federal funding records, governance documents, and correspondence with OCR.

### What Title IX Requires of Covered Missouri Religious Schools

A Missouri religious private school subject to Title IX carries the same substantive obligations as any public school district:

- Designating a **Title IX Coordinator** and publishing that coordinator's name and contact information in school materials
- Adopting a written **grievance procedure** that provides for prompt and equitable resolution of sexual harassment and sexual violence complaints
- **Responding promptly and effectively** to reports or known instances of sexual misconduct by teachers, coaches, administrators, counselors, clergy-educators, or other school personnel
- **Investigating complaints** through a process that is fair, impartial, and not delegated to the perpetrator's religious superior or institutional employer
- **Imposing remedies** designed to end the harassment, prevent its recurrence, and address its effects on the survivor's educational access
- **Not retaliating** against students, parents, or other reporters who bring forward sexual misconduct complaints

Failure to meet these obligations is not merely an administrative deficiency. Under the **deliberate indifference Title IX** standard, a school's clearly unreasonable response to known abuse is the basis for civil liability in a **Title IX lawsuit Missouri** survivors can bring in federal court.

## The Legal Standard: Deliberate Indifference in Missouri Religious School Cases

### What Deliberate Indifference Means Under Federal Law

Two controlling U.S. Supreme Court decisions establish the standard for institutional Title IX liability in teacher-on-student and student-on-student abuse cases.

For **teacher-on-student or coach-on-student abuse**, the Supreme Court's decision in ***Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School District***, 524 U.S. 274 (1998), requires a plaintiff to establish:

1. An official of the school — meaning an administrator, principal, superintendent, diocesan official, or other person with actual authority to take corrective action — had **actual knowledge** of the abuse, **and**
2. That official responded with **deliberate indifference** — a response that was clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances

For **student-on-student sexual harassment**, the Supreme Court's decision in ***Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education***, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), requires:

1. A school official with authority to address the harassment had **actual knowledge** of the conduct
2. The school's response was **deliberately indifferent**
3. The harassment was **severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive** — meaning it effectively denied the survivor equal access to educational opportunities
