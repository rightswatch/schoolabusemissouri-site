## What Title IX Is and Why It Matters to Missouri Survivors

### The Federal Law That Holds School Districts Liable for Sex Discrimination in Missouri

Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, 20 U.S.C. § 1681, carries one direct command: no person shall be excluded from participation in, denied the benefits of, or subjected to discrimination under any educational program or activity receiving federal financial assistance on the basis of sex.

The Supreme Court has held that sexual harassment and sexual abuse by school employees or students constitute sex discrimination under Title IX. That ruling carries a practical consequence most families do not initially understand: **when a district fails to respond appropriately to abuse, the district itself can be held financially liable** — not just the individual teacher, coach, or counselor who caused the harm.

Understanding **how to file a Title IX lawsuit in Missouri** begins with understanding this distinction. Your family's claim is not limited to pursuing the individual perpetrator. A **Title IX lawsuit Missouri** parents file targets the institution that empowered, ignored, or protected that perpetrator.

### Which Missouri Schools Are Covered Under Title IX

Nearly every educational institution in Missouri receives federal funding and is therefore covered by Title IX:

- Every K-12 public school district, from Kansas City Public Schools to Rockwood R-VI School District to Springfield R-XII School District to Hazelwood School District
- Every public university, including the University of Missouri-Columbia, Missouri State University, and St. Louis Community College
- Charter schools operating in St. Louis City, Kansas City, and across the state that receive federal funding
- Private schools — including private K-12 academies and faith-based institutions — that accept federal funding in any form, such as federal Title I funds or Pell Grant-eligible tuition assistance

**School district liability Missouri** law recognizes extends across all of these institutions. Federal funding coverage is broad, and few Missouri schools fall entirely outside Title IX's reach.

## Who Commits School Sexual Abuse in Missouri — and Why the District May Be Responsible

### The Roles Missouri Districts Are Required to Supervise

A district's legal liability under Title IX and Missouri state law depends substantially on who caused the harm and what administrators, supervisors, and Title IX coordinators knew. These are the categories that appear most frequently in Missouri school sexual abuse cases handled by a **school sexual abuse lawyer Missouri** families contact after institutional failures.

**Teachers**

A teacher who sexually abuses a student exploits the authority and institutional access the district granted them. Missouri teachers are licensed by the Missouri Department of Elementary and Secondary Education (DESE) and are subject to background check requirements, fitness-to-teach standards, and mandatory reporting obligations under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 210.115. When a teacher grooms and abuses a student — exchanging private messages, arranging after-school meetings, or escalating contact during tutoring sessions — the district faces potential liability under both Title IX and Missouri civil law if a principal, assistant principal, or Title IX coordinator knew or should have known about warning signs and failed to act.

A particularly dangerous and well-documented pattern in Missouri involves a licensed teacher who receives a complaint, resigns before formal discipline concludes, and is hired by a neighboring district — sometimes within the same school year — because the prior district never reported to DESE and never sought license revocation. DESE has authority under Missouri law to revoke, suspend, or refuse to renew a teaching certificate based on immoral conduct, and a district's failure to initiate that process after substantiated sexual misconduct is itself evidence of institutional indifference. **If your child was abused by a teacher who had prior complaints at another Missouri district, that history is potentially critical evidence — and it must be preserved and documented before records are purged or witnesses become unavailable.**

**Coaches**

Athletic coaches often have access to students that classroom teachers do not — in locker rooms, during out-of-town travel, during early-morning conditioning sessions, and in one-on-one skill development meetings. The power imbalance between a varsity coach and a student athlete is well-documented, and courts take it seriously. Courts applying the **deliberate indifference Title IX** standard established in *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), have examined cases where coaches made sexual comments to athletes, engaged in unwanted touching during training, or progressed to criminal sexual conduct — and where district administrators either ignored complaints or actively protected a winning program's staffing.

In Missouri, a coach who is also a licensed teacher carries dual obligations: the Title IX compliance obligations the district owes, and the individual mandatory reporting obligation under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 210.115. When a coach witnesses or has reason to suspect abuse of a student by another staff member and fails to report, that coach has committed a Class A misdemeanor under Missouri law — and the district's knowledge of that coach's inaction becomes relevant to deliberate indifference analysis.

**School Counselors and Administrators**

Students are directed to counselors during their most vulnerable moments — after a family crisis, following a traumatic event, or when struggling academically. When a licensed school counselor exploits that vulnerability through sexual abuse or harassment, the betrayal is compounded by the therapeutic nature of the relationship. When a building principal or assistant principal engages in sexual misconduct, the district's failure to supervise carries serious legal implications under both Title IX and Missouri supervisory liability standards.

Licensed school counselors in Missouri are mandatory reporters under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 210.115. A counselor who receives a disclosure from a student about abuse by a teacher, coach, or other staff member and fails to report to the Missouri Children's Division or law enforcement has committed an independent criminal offense — and that failure simultaneously strips the district of any credible claim that it responded reasonably under *Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School District*, 524 U.S. 274 (1998).

**Bus Drivers**

A bus driver has unsupervised access to students during transport, often at the beginning and end of every school day. Districts that employ bus drivers directly — rather than contracting with a third-party transportation company — retain supervisory responsibility over those employees. Prior complaints about a bus driver's conduct with students, when known to a transportation director or district administrator and ignored, support a **deliberate indifference Title IX** claim under both federal doctrine and Missouri supervisory liability law.

**Other Students — Peer-on-Peer Harassment and Assault**

Title IX covers severe, pervasive peer sexual harassment and student-on-student sexual assault. When one student sexually assaults or persistently harasses another, the district can be held liable if an appropriate person — a principal, dean of students, or Title IX coordinator — had actual knowledge of the harassment and responded with deliberate indifference. Under *Davis*, the harassment must be severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive, and must effectively deny the victim access to educational opportunities. Districts invoke this demanding standard to avoid accountability far more often than it actually shields them.

In peer harassment cases in Missouri, a separate layer of accountability exists: if a school employee learns of the assault or harassment and has reasonable cause to believe abuse occurred, that employee is a mandatory reporter under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 210.115 and must report to the Children's Division or law enforcement — regardless of whether the perpetrator is an adult or another student. A **mandatory reporter failure Missouri** families can document creates both criminal exposure for the individual employee and powerful evidence in support of the district's Title IX deliberate indifference liability.
