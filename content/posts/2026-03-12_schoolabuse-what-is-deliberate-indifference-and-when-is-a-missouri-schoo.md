## What Title IX Requires of Missouri Schools

Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 is a federal civil rights law. Its operative text is direct: **"No person in the United States shall, on the basis of sex, be excluded from the participation in, be denied the benefits of, or be subjected to discrimination under any education program or activity receiving Federal financial assistance."**

Sexual harassment, sexual assault, and sexual abuse are all forms of sex discrimination under Title IX. Every Missouri public school district — from Kansas City Public Schools and St. Louis Public Schools to Francis Howell R-III, Springfield R-XII, Columbia Public Schools, and Joplin R-VIII — receives federal financial assistance. Title IX applies to all of them, from kindergarten through graduate school.

When a school fails to respond appropriately to known sexual abuse or harassment, it can be held liable in a private lawsuit for money damages. That liability turns on whether the school acted with **deliberate indifference** to a known risk or known misconduct.

## The Deliberate Indifference Standard: What It Means and Why It Controls Your Case

### Where the Standard Comes From

The U.S. Supreme Court established the deliberate indifference standard in two decisions every Missouri Title IX case turns on:

- ***Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School District* (1998)** — teacher-on-student abuse
- ***Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education* (1999)** — student-on-student harassment

These cases define when a school district or university can be held liable in damages under Title IX. Every Missouri Title IX case — whether filed in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Missouri in St. Louis or the Western District of Missouri in Kansas City — is measured against them.

### What Deliberate Indifference Actually Means

Deliberate indifference exists when a school's response to known sexual misconduct is **clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances**. It sits above simple negligence — a school is not automatically liable just because it could have done more — but it does not require proof that officials acted with malice or specific intent to harm a student.

A school acts with deliberate indifference when it:

- **Knew** about sexual abuse, assault, harassment, or a substantial risk of such harm, **and**
- **Responded in a way that was clearly unreasonable** — or **failed to respond at all**

A school that investigates promptly, implements protective measures, and imposes proportionate discipline has generally not acted with deliberate indifference, even if the outcome was imperfect. The standard is not perfection. It is **reasonable response under the circumstances**.

### The "Actual Knowledge" Requirement: Who at the School Had to Know

For a Missouri school to face Title IX liability, an **appropriate person** — an official with actual authority to take corrective action — must have had genuine awareness of the misconduct or the substantial risk of it. Rumors circulating among students in the hallways of Hazelwood East High School or Parkway Central Middle School do not satisfy this standard. Knowledge must reach someone empowered to act.

In Missouri K-12 districts, appropriate persons typically include:

- Building principals and assistant principals
- District-level administrators and superintendents
- Title IX coordinators
- School board members in certain circumstances

Appropriate persons at Missouri's public school districts — including Hickman Mills C-1, Independence School District, Parkway C-2, Ladue School District, Webster Groves School District, Affton School District, and Mehlville R-IX — carry the institutional authority that triggers the school's obligation to respond.

At Missouri universities — including the University of Missouri campuses at Columbia, Kansas City, St. Louis, and Rolla (Missouri S&T); Missouri State University in Springfield; Truman State University in Kirksville; Washington University in St. Louis; and Saint Louis University — appropriate persons typically include Title IX coordinators, deans, department chairs, and other administrative officials with authority to act.

**For families:** If a survivor reports abuse to a teacher, counselor, or coach at Fort Zumwalt North High School or to a University of Missouri dormitory resident advisor who lacks Title IX authority to take corrective action, that report may not trigger institutional "actual knowledge" for purposes of federal liability. But many of those same employees are **mandatory reporters under Missouri law** — and their failure to report to authorities triggers separate legal consequences discussed below.

## What Schools Must Do Once They Have Actual Knowledge

Once an appropriate school official — the Title IX Coordinator at St. Louis Public Schools, the Superintendent of Springfield R-XII, the Dean of Students at Missouri State University — has actual knowledge of sexual misconduct, a legally adequate response requires:

- Opening a prompt, impartial, and thorough investigation
- Implementing interim protective measures — separating the accused from the survivor, adjusting schedules, providing counseling access, placing an accused employee on administrative leave
- Notifying the survivor of their rights and available support services
- Reaching a reasonable determination based on a complete investigation
- Taking proportionate disciplinary action when misconduct is substantiated
- Monitoring the situation to prevent recurrence

### What Schools Cannot Do Without Crossing Into Deliberate Indifference

Deliberate indifference is most clearly established when school officials — whether a Rockwood School District principal, a Kansas City Public Schools administrator, or a University of Missouri Title IX coordinator — do one or more of the following:

- **Ignore reports entirely** — receive a complaint and file it with no investigation
- **Investigate superficially** and reach predetermined conclusions
- **Delay indefinitely** without taking any interim protective action
- **Discipline or burden the survivor** — through punitive schedule changes, removal from activities, or administrative ostracism
- **Actively discourage reporting** — telling students or parents at Lindbergh Schools or Kirkwood School District that complaining will make things worse
- **Return an accused teacher or coach to the classroom** at Hazelwood School District or Francis Howell R-III while a complaint is pending, without any protective measures in place
- **Reassign a perpetrator to another school** — moving a coach from North Kansas City High School to Winnetonka High School rather than investigating or disciplining them — a pattern Missouri families should document carefully
- **Fail to report known abuse** to the Missouri Children's Division or law enforcement as required by state law

## Types of Perpetrators and How Liability Differs

### Teacher, Coach, and Administrator Abuse in Missouri Schools

When a teacher, coach, counselor, or administrator at a Missouri school — from a Joplin R-VIII elementary teacher to a University of Missouri athletics department coach — sexually abuses a student, Title IX liability focuses on what school officials knew and how they responded.

**Before the abuse:** Warning signs that went unaddressed can support a deliberate indifference claim. Courts examine whether school officials at districts like Parkway C-2, Wentzville R-IV, or Lee's Summit R-VII were aware of:

- A teacher spending excessive time alone with students in a classroom at Eureka High School or a weight room at Blue Springs South High School
- Gift-giving or private communication through personal devices between a Hazelwood Central High School coach and students
- Physical contact that other employees or parents at Rockwood Summit High School had flagged
- Prior complaints from students or families at Affton High School or Mehlville High School that were dismissed without investigation

**After the abuse:** Once a school knows an employee has abused a student — whether that school is Ladue Horton Watkins High School, a Kansas City Public Schools middle school, or Truman State University — the obligation to investigate, discipline, and prevent continued access is unambiguous. Any failure at that stage is difficult to defend.

Missouri school districts have faced legal exposure in cases involving:

- **Band directors and music teachers** at schools including Kirkwood High School and Fort Zumwalt East High School who used private lesson settings for grooming and abuse
- **Athletic coaches** at programs including those at Webster Groves High School, Lee's Summit High School, and University of Missouri-Columbia who exploited authority, travel settings, and physical access to abuse student athletes
- **School counselors** at districts including Independence School District and Hickman Mills C-1 who exploited trust relationships with vulnerable students
- **Principals and vice principals** at schools including those in Springfield R-XII and St. Louis Public Schools who used disciplinary authority to isolate and access students
- **Bus drivers** employed by Mehlville R-IX, Rockwood School District, and Francis Howell R-III with access to students in isolated settings
- **Special education teachers and aides** at districts including Special School District of St. Louis County who abused students with disabilities whose communication challenges made disclosure difficult

Each of these settings shares a recognizable structure: **authority, isolation, and access**. When school officials receive warning signs about any employee in any of these roles and do nothing, that inaction is the foundation of a deliberate indifference claim.
